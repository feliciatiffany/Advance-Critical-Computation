Project Description
This project is a browser-based poem generator inspired by the magic of street poets and the wandering thoughts we all carry. The idea sparked after I met @peoplearepoetry not too long ago in Union Square. I was instantly drawn to his quiet presence in the corner of the subway, typing away on an old typewriter. What fascinated me was not just the poems, but the process: how he’d ask for a theme or feeling, sometimes offering his own question if you weren’t sure. People could respond with anything — something specific, something abstract — and he’d begin typing, slowly and with intention.
What made him unforgettable was the way he explained each poem after finishing. At first, I didn’t fully understand them. But with each reading, I became more captivated. It’s hard to describe the feeling exactly, but the rhythm, the diction, the imagery — it all started to make sense, and it resonated deeply. I came to love the poems he wrote for me. Maybe even got a little addicted to the way they unraveled something I couldn’t quite name.
From that experience, I wanted to create an online version of this poetic exchange — a space where people might begin to love poetry the way I did. Not just as beautiful language, but as art: full of thought, layers, and intention. I hope this project becomes a gentle introduction to poetry, a small door for people to step through.
Visually, the project centers around the old typewriter — to reference history, intimacy, and the experience of someone writing something just for you. As the poem appears, each word is typed slowly, pausing randomly like a poet pausing to think. The interface evokes the texture of vintage paper, the quiet presence of a page waiting for thought.
The interaction begins with a single open-ended prompt:
"What’s in your mind lately?"
The participant enters a word, thought, or feeling. That input acts like a divination, triggering a poem that relates to it, either thematically or abstractly. The text is typed out on screen through a vintage typewriter animation, and the poem’s author is credited at the end. If the match feels random, that’s part of the point — like any good divination, it invites reflection. It’s not about perfect relevance, but about what you bring to it as a reader.
This project won’t be as alive or intimate as sitting across from a real poet like @peoplearepoetry, but I hope it still sparks recognition, wonder, and a growing affection for poems in the people who encounter it.
